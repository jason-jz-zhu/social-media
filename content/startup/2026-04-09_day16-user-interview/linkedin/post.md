Twitch didn't grow by listening to viewers. They grew by ignoring them.

Emmett Shear spent years talking to the WRONG users before figuring out that WHO you interview matters more than WHAT you ask. Twitch's entire strategy shifted when they stopped surveying viewers and started talking to broadcasters — the people who actually drove the platform.

I'm revisiting YC's startup lectures against 10+ years of building data and AI products, and Shear's "How to Run a User Interview" is the most tactical lecture in the series so far. His framework splits users into three groups that give you COMPLETELY different feedback: current users (known pain), competitor users (why they don't use you), and non-users (what's blocking them from the market entirely). Most teams only talk to group one.

The principle that hit hardest: don't show your product during interviews. The moment you show it, you're polluting the data. You stop learning what's already in their heads and start testing your assumptions. User interviews aren't demos. They're archaeological digs.

And the ultimate validation? Not "do they like it." Not even "will they use the demo." It's whether they'll PAY. Money is the only signal that cuts through social desirability bias. I've seen this in every data product I've built — surveys say yes, budgets say no.
