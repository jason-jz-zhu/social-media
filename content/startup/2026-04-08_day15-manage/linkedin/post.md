You can fire someone with kindness and still destroy your culture.

Ben Horowitz argues that demoting someone — the "nice" alternative to firing — is actually worse. The person keeps 1.5% equity they didn't earn at their current level. They lose authority with their team. And everyone else learns that poor performance gets rewarded with a second chance instead of accountability.

I'm revisiting YC's startup lectures against 10+ years of building data and AI products, and Horowitz's "How to Manage" talk reframed something I thought I understood. His core principle: every management decision must be seen through EVERYONE's eyes — not just yours and the person in the room, but everyone NOT in the room.

The raise example sealed it. If you give raises to people who ask, you're systematically punishing the best engineers who just put their heads down and ship. So you build process — formal evaluation cycles — not as bureaucracy, but as protection against invisible unfairness.

Here's what connects this to yesterday's talk: Rabois said simplify everything. Horowitz says add process. They sound contradictory, but they're not. Simplification removes unnecessary steps. Process standardizes necessary ones. The real complexity comes from having NO process — ad hoc decisions that create chaos.
