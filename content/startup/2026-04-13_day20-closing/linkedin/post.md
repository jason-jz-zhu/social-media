One thousand days. Almost three years of nothing.

I just finished all 20 lectures in YC's startup course — revisiting each one against 10+ years of building data and AI products — and Sam Altman's closing lecture is the one that hit hardest. Not because it introduced new frameworks. Because it said the quiet part out loud: the journey is brutally long, most founders lose focus before they fail, and the shift from building product to building company happens whether you're ready or not.

At 25 employees, everything changes. You stop writing code and start writing org charts. You stop shipping features and start managing managers. The founder who got you here is not the person who runs what's here now — but you have to become that person anyway.

The counterintuitive advice: NEVER hire a professional CEO to replace yourself. And avoid M&A conversations entirely — they kill more companies than competition does. The distraction alone is lethal.

After 20 days of studying these lectures, here is what I believe: the best startup advice from 2014 still holds in 2026 — but AI compresses every timeline. The 25-person threshold hits at 10. The trough of sorrow is shorter but steeper. The founder's psychology matters more than ever because the tools are so powerful that execution speed is no longer the bottleneck. Judgment is.
