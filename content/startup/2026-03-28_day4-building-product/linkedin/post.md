Adora Cheung pivoted 12 TIMES before landing on Homejoy. Then she did something most founders would never do — she went undercover as a house cleaner.

Not to "empathize with users." To become one.

She scrubbed toilets, mopped floors, and dealt with clients who wouldn't pay. Her 13th idea became a $37M company, and it only clicked because she stopped theorizing and started sweating.

I'm revisiting her YC lecture against 10 years of building data and AI products, and her "honesty curve" framework hit hard. Who gives you the most honest feedback? Not your mom. Not your friends. Strangers who PAY you. Cash doesn't lie.

In AI, we talk endlessly about "user research" and "feedback loops." But Adora's point is sharper: don't automate what you haven't done manually. Don't build dashboards for workflows you've never lived through. The founders who skip the manual phase build what she calls "Frankenstein products" — stitched together from assumptions, not reality.

Her one-sentence test is deceptively simple: if you can't describe your product in one sentence, you don't understand it yet. "$20/hour, get your place cleaned." That's it. That clarity took 12 failed pivots to reach.

Temporary brokenness beats permanent paralysis. Ship it broken. Fix it live.
