DoorDash's first tech stack was a landing page, a PDF menu, and a personal phone number. Built in one hour.

No algorithms. No driver network. No backend. The founders delivered the food THEMSELVES, took support calls during Stanford lectures, and tracked drivers with Find My Friends.

I'm revisiting YC's lecture series against a decade of building data and AI products, and Stanley Tang's story reframed something I keep seeing in tech: we over-engineer before we even know if anyone wants what we're building. Tang spent ONE HOUR testing a hypothesis that became a $70B company. Most teams spend months building infrastructure for problems nobody has.

Walker Williams reinforces this from Teespring: his first 50 users took DAYS of in-person meetings to close. He describes early traction like pushing a boulder uphill — steepest at the start. But those first painful users became the longest-term champions.

The counterintuitive part: doing unscalable things isn't a phase you graduate from. It's a competitive advantage you should hold onto as LONG as possible. Don't stop voluntarily. Let it be ripped from you.
