## Cross-Lecture Insight #1: The Paradox of Starting Small

This was the pattern that hit me hardest when I looked at the week as a whole.

Sam Altman (Day 1) says the best startups begin with ideas that address markets most people think are too small. The examples are now famous — Facebook started at one college. Airbnb started with air mattresses. Google was the 13th search engine nobody asked for.

Peter Thiel (Day 5) takes this further with his concentric circles model. Don't just start small — start *absurdly* small. Amazon started with books. PayPal started with 20,000 eBay power sellers. Not "online payments" — twenty thousand specific humans. Dominate that group completely, then expand to the adjacent circle.

And Adora Cheung (Day 4) provides the mechanism for HOW you start small. You become a cog in the machine. You work in the industry at the ground level. Her one-sentence test — can you describe your product in one sentence? — is the litmus test for whether you've actually narrowed down enough.

Here's what none of these lectures say explicitly, but what becomes clear when you line them up: **starting small isn't a compromise. It's a strategy.** The smallness is the feature. A tiny market gives you room to learn, to iterate, to build the domain expertise that becomes your moat when you expand. Thiel would call that moat a monopoly. Graham would call it genuine understanding. Cheung would call it embodied knowledge from doing the work yourself.

In my MBA classes at Stern, we studied market sizing and TAM analysis as if the goal was always to find the biggest possible market. YC's message is the opposite: find the smallest possible market where you can build something people love, and let the bigness come later.

## Cross-Lecture Insight #2: The War on Planning

The second pattern I noticed is how aggressively these lectures push back against traditional planning.

Paul Graham (Day 3) says startup ideas should come from side projects, not strategic brainstorming. When you deliberately try to think of startup ideas, you filter for things that "sound like startup ideas" rather than things that solve real problems. He calls the alternative "playing house" — going through the motions of startup life without building anything meaningful. Getting an LLC. Designing business cards. Attending networking events.

Adora Cheung (Day 4) echoes this from the product side. Her "honesty curve" framework says the most honest feedback comes from paying strangers, not from planning documents or friendly advisors. Her Frankenstein product warning is essentially an anti-planning argument: don't build a roadmap based on aggregated user requests. Nail one thing first.

Sam Altman (Day 2) attacks planning from the execution side. Say no to 97 out of 100 things. The best founders are "almost pathologically focused." They can articulate exactly what they're NOT doing, and that list is always longer than what they are doing.

And here's where my MBA brain catches fire. Business school teaches you to plan. Extensively. Market analysis, competitive mapping, financial projections, scenario planning. It's all planning. And YC is basically saying: most of that is theater. The plans don't survive contact with reality. The only planning that matters is deciding what NOT to do.

I don't think business school is wrong, exactly. The frameworks are useful for established companies navigating known markets. But for anything genuinely new — a startup, a new product line, an internal innovation project — the YC approach feels more honest. You can't plan your way to insight. You have to build your way there.

## Cross-Lecture Insight #3: The 100-User Obsession

Altman's framework from Day 1 — "it is better to build something that 100 people love than something that 10,000 people kind of like" — kept echoing through the entire week.

Thiel (Day 5) operationalized it: find the smallest market where you can achieve monopoly-level dominance. That market might be 20,000 eBay sellers or 10,000 Harvard students.

Graham (Day 3) explained the psychology: users are like sharks. They detect whether your product is real. You cannot trick them with growth hacks or clever marketing. The only thing that works is genuinely solving their problem.

Cheung (Day 4) explained the mechanics: get paying strangers. Even one dollar. That dollar comes with feedback more honest than a thousand friendly conversations.

And Altman (Day 2) explained the execution: your first employees should multiply velocity, not divide it. Stay small. Be proud of how few people you have. Because when you're small, you can be obsessive about those 100 users in a way that a company of 200 never can.

The 100-user obsession isn't just a nice framework. It's a survival strategy. And in the AI era, where a two-person team with the right tools can serve thousands of users, there's even less excuse for not starting with a laser focus on a tiny group of people you serve extraordinarily well.

## The MBA vs. YC Divide

I want to be honest about something: this week made me uncomfortable. Not because the lectures were bad — they're exceptional. But because they challenged a significant portion of what I've invested two years learning in business school.

At Stern, I've studied market sizing, competitive analysis, strategic planning, organizational design, and financial modeling. All of it assumes a level of predictability and structure that YC treats as fiction. Paul Graham says you don't need to know about startups to succeed at one — you need to know about users. Thiel says the MBA framework of "find a big market and compete" is literally the recipe for failure. Adora Cheung says user research methodology is theater compared to actually becoming a cog in the industry.

Here's my synthesis after a week of sitting with this tension: **MBA training optimizes for reducing downside risk. YC training optimizes for maximizing upside potential.** They're solving different problems.

If you're managing an existing business — allocating resources, entering new markets, managing a P&L — MBA frameworks are invaluable. You need the rigor. You need the structure. But if you're creating something genuinely new, the YC mindset is more useful. You need the chaos tolerance. You need the willingness to start small and ugly and learn by doing.

The best position, I'm starting to believe, is having both toolkits and knowing when to switch. The MBA brain keeps you from making stupid financial decisions. The YC brain keeps you from making cowardly product decisions.

## What Changed in My Thinking This Week

Three specific shifts:

**1. I stopped romanticizing big ideas.** Before this week, I unconsciously associated "great startup idea" with "big vision." After Day 1 and Day 5, I see that the best ideas start looking small, specific, and even slightly embarrassing. The bigness comes later — if you earn it.

**2. I stopped treating my data/AI background as separate from startup thinking.** After a decade in tech, I had this mental partition: "my day job expertise" in one box, "startup aspirations" in another. These lectures demolished that partition. My domain expertise IS the startup advantage. Knowing data systems, knowing AI limitations, knowing what breaks in production — that's not a resume line. That's a moat.

**3. I started questioning my instinct to plan.** I'm a planner by nature. Spreadsheets, roadmaps, decision matrices — I love them all. Graham's lecture in particular made me realize that planning can be a form of procrastination. The most important thing I can do for any new project is talk to users and build something, not plan the perfect approach.

## The Story I Didn't Tell on Medium

Here's something I haven't shared in the daily articles, because it's too personal for that format.

The chat-to-data project I mentioned in the Day 1 article — the tool that lets non-technical users query databases using natural language — I've been working on it nights and weekends for almost a year. It started as a hack week project at work, evolved into a weekend side project, and has slowly become the thing I think about most.

After this week of lectures, I ran it through every framework:

- **Altman's test:** Can I not NOT do it? Yes. I literally can't stop working on it. I open the codebase on weekends without planning to. I sketch features during MBA lectures. That's the signal Moskovitz described — the almost irrational pull toward a problem.
- **Graham's test:** Did it start as a side project born from genuine frustration? Yes. I built it because I was tired of writing SQL for people who just wanted answers. For years, I've been the human translation layer between business users and databases. "Can you pull last quarter's numbers?" became "let me write the query for you" hundreds of times. The project started because I wanted to automate myself out of that job.
- **Cheung's test:** Have I been the cog? Yes. I've been the one manually writing queries for non-technical colleagues for years. I know the pain intimately — the back-and-forth clarifications, the "actually can you add this column," the misunderstandings about what a metric means. I know which questions are easy to translate and which ones require three rounds of clarification. No amount of user research could give me that.
- **Thiel's test:** Is there a small market I can dominate? This is where I'm stuck. "Data analysts who need to serve business users" is too broad. I need to find my 20,000 eBay sellers — the absurdly specific group where this tool is life-changing. Maybe it's product analysts at mid-stage startups who spend 40% of their week fielding ad hoc SQL requests. Maybe it's marketing teams at e-commerce companies who want to self-serve their own data. I don't know yet, and that uncertainty is uncomfortable.

What I haven't done — and what this week convinced me I need to do — is get a stranger to pay for it. Not a friend. Not a colleague. A stranger with a credit card. That's the top of Cheung's honesty curve, and I've been avoiding it because I'm afraid of what honest feedback will feel like. What if the product that works great for me, a data person who understands the underlying schema, falls apart the moment a true non-technical user touches it? That gap between "works for the builder" and "works for the user" is where most AI products go to die, and I know that from experience.

Week 1 of this series gave me the frameworks. Now I need the courage to apply them.

## The AI/Data Angle: What YC Got Right Before AI Existed

One thing I keep coming back to: these lectures are from 2014. Pre-GPT. Pre-transformer. Pre-everything that defines the current tech landscape. And yet the core frameworks haven't aged at all — if anything, AI has made them more relevant.

Altman's "100 users who love you" is easier to serve with AI tools but harder to find because every market is noisier. Thiel's monopoly framework applies perfectly to AI startups: the horizontal "AI for everything" companies are in perfect competition, while the vertical "AI for radiology at rural hospitals" companies are building monopolies. Graham's "just learn" philosophy matters more than ever because AI has made building cheap while leaving understanding expensive.

The single biggest trap I see in AI right now — and I see it because I spend $450/month on these tools and talk to builders daily — is the belief that AI tools eliminate the need for domain expertise. They don't. They amplify it. A domain expert with AI is unstoppable. A generalist with AI is just faster at building things nobody wants. Every lecture this week reinforced that insight from a different angle.
