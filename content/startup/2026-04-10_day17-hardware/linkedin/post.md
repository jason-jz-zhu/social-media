Jawbone asked people "Would you buy a $199 wireless speaker?" and got 0% interest.

Then they asked "How do you listen to music with other people?" and built a product that created an entire category — going from 0% to 78% market share.

I'm revisiting YC's startup lectures against 10+ years of building data and AI products, and Hosain Rahman's lecture on hardware design contains a principle that applies FAR beyond hardware: don't ask users to validate your idea. Understand their behavior, then YOU innovate.

This connects directly to yesterday's lecture on user interviews. Emmett Shear said don't show your product. Rahman says don't even describe it. Instead, ask about BEHAVIOR. "How do you listen to music?" not "Do you want this speaker?" Apple didn't say "digital music player." They said "1000 songs in your pocket." The question you ask determines the product you build.

Rahman's other framework that stuck: hardware is not a device. It's a system — hardware + software + data. Track heart rate (hardware), understand what it means in context (software + data), then tell users what to DO about it. Data alone is useless. "Heart rate 75" means nothing without context.
