A founder lost $500,000 because they incorporated in the wrong state and botched their conversion to Delaware.

Not because of a bad product. Not because of a bad team. Because of paperwork.

I'm revisiting YC's startup lectures against 10+ years of building data and AI products, and Lecture 18 by Kirsty Nathoo (YC CFO) and Carolynn Levy (YC Partner/Lawyer) is the one most founders would skip — and the one that saves the most money.

Their core message: boring legal and financial hygiene is what separates companies that survive fundraising and acquisition from companies that implode during due diligence. Incorporate in Delaware. File your 83(b) election within 30 days — deals have literally blown up over this. Use standard 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff, even for solo founders. Pay yourselves at least minimum wage. And treat investor money like someone else's money — because it is.

The framework that stuck: every top YC company splits equity roughly equally. Ideas are worth $0. Execution is everything. The moment you give a cofounder 5% "because it was your idea," you've created a ticking time bomb.
