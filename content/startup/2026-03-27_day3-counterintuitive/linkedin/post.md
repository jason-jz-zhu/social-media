Paul Graham says "growth hacks" are bullshit.

After 10+ years building data and AI products, I think he's being generous.

Revisiting YC's Lecture 3, Graham drops a metaphor that reframes everything: startups are counterintuitive like skiing. Your instincts are WRONG. Lean forward when every fiber of your body screams to lean back.

The one exception? Trusting your gut about PEOPLE. Graham says you can't predict who will succeed as a founder. But you CAN tell when someone feels off. Trust that signal.

Here's the part that hit hardest:

Users are like sharks. They don't care about your pitch deck, your growth hack, your viral loop. They detect whether your product is meat or not meat. That's it. No amount of gaming the system changes this.

The best startup ideas? They don't come from brainstorming sessions or hackathons. They come from side projects born of genuine curiosity. Yahoo, Google, Facebook, Apple — all started as things the founders built because they couldn't NOT build them.

Graham's ultimate advice to future founders isn't "learn to code" or "find a cofounder." It's two words: JUST LEARN. Get deeply good at something. The ideas will find you.

From my own experience in AI and data: the best products I've worked on were never the ones we "strategized" into existence. They were the ones that started as someone's weekend obsession.
