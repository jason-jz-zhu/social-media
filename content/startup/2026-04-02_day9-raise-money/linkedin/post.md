a16z passed on Airbnb in the first round. The deal that became one of the greatest venture returns in history — and one of the smartest firms in Silicon Valley said no.

Marc Andreessen explains why: they were looking for lack of weakness instead of EXTREME STRENGTH. Airbnb had obvious flaws. But it also had something no other company had — users who were obsessively, irrationally in love with the product.

I'm revisiting YC's startup lecture series against a decade of building data and AI products, and this framing hit me harder than any fundraising advice I've heard. Andreessen, Ron Conway, and Parker Conrad lay out a model where fundraising is actually the EASIEST thing a founder does — if the business is genuinely great.

The uncomfortable truth: VCs see 4,000 companies a year. They invest in 200. Only 15 generate 97% of returns. If you're optimizing your pitch deck instead of your product, you're playing the wrong game.

Conway's filter is brutal. He needs to understand what you do in the FIRST SENTENCE. If he can't, you're out.
