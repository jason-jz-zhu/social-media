# AI Looks Exactly Like the Electricity Revolution 100 Years Ago

## Paul David wrote the answer 30 years ago. We just didn't listen.

It's not a metaphor. It's the same pattern. And a Stanford economist named Paul David wrote the playbook in 1990.

## The paper that quietly predicted everything

In 1990, a Stanford economist named **Paul David** published a paper called *"The Dynamo and the Computer: An Historical Perspective on the Modern Productivity Paradox."*

The paradox he was trying to explain was this: it was 1990, computers had been in offices for two decades, and yet U.S. productivity numbers hadn't budged. Solow's famous quip — *"we see computers everywhere except in the productivity statistics"* — was the central mystery of the decade.

David's answer was: **this has happened before. Look at electricity.**

Then he laid out the numbers. And the numbers are what should stop you.

- Commercial electric dynamos existed from **the 1880s**.
- By **1900**, roughly 5% of American factory horsepower was electric.
- U.S. manufacturing total factor productivity (TFP) grew **less than 1% per year for three decades** — from the 1890s straight through World War I.
- Then, between **1919 and 1929**, TFP suddenly exploded to **more than 5% per year**.
- Over that same decade, electricity's share of factory power jumped from about 50% to nearly 80%.

Read that again. **Thirty years of "where's the productivity?" — then a decade of explosive gains.**

The technology hadn't gotten dramatically better. What changed was how factories were built around it.

That's the whole game. And it's the game we're in.

## The five uncanny parallels

Once you start looking, the parallels between electrification (1880–1930) and AI (2020–?) get almost embarrassing.

### 1. Edison was pushed out of his own company. The "inventor" lost.

By 1892, the man whose name is synonymous with electricity — Thomas Edison — was **squeezed out of Edison General Electric** in a J.P. Morgan-arranged merger with Thomson-Houston. He resigned from the GE board after one meeting and sold his shares by 1894. Within two years of the merger, GE itself had **pivoted to AC** — Edison's rival technology.

Why? Not sentiment. **Unit economics.** Edison's DC needed a power station roughly every mile because voltage dropped so fast over copper. Westinghouse and Tesla's AC could be stepped up to high voltage, sent across states, then stepped back down. AC just *cost less per delivered kilowatt-hour*.

The market didn't care who invented what first. It cared who could deliver electricity for cheaper.

**Now look at 2026.** Foundation-model labs — the "inventors" of this generation — are burning cash at scales that would make Edison blush. The people making actual money are the operators and integrators who figured out cheaper unit economics: cheaper inference, better caching, smaller distilled models good enough for the job.

Are today's frontier labs the next generation of Edisons — first to invent, last to profit? I don't know. But history has cast this role before, and it wasn't the inventor who won.

### 2. Tesla invented AC and never made a dime

If Edison is the tragic first-mover, **Nikola Tesla is the tragic inventor**. In 1888 he sold his polyphase AC patents to Westinghouse for about **$60,000 plus royalties**. Then, when Westinghouse hit financial trouble a few years later, Tesla — famously — **tore up the royalty contract** to save the company.

He forfeited what would have been one of the most valuable IP streams of the 20th century. He died penniless in a New York hotel room in 1943, feeding pigeons.

**The people writing the papers are almost never the people cashing the checks.**

Look around the AI world. The researchers whose names are on the papers that made transformers and RLHF possible — how many of them own meaningful equity in the companies now valued at hundreds of billions? A handful. The value flowed to the commercializers, the ones who built the products and the distribution.

This is not new. It has never been new.

### 3. The 30-year productivity void

This is the point I want you to sit with, because it's the whole essay in one number.

**30 years.**

From the invention of the commercial dynamo to the explosion of productivity, factories in the U.S. bought electric motors, installed them, ran them, and got *essentially no measurable productivity boost*. Not a little boost. None.

Then, between 1919 and 1929, everything unlocked at once.

Now check your calendar. ChatGPT launched in **November 2022**. We are, roughly, three years in.

Every McKinsey "companies aren't getting ROI from AI" report, every leaked email about "we're going to have to limit Claude usage," every VC memo asking "where's the productivity?" — congratulations, **you are living in year three of a thirty-year process**. The absence of aggregate productivity gains is not evidence that AI is a bubble. It's evidence that AI is a general-purpose technology, behaving exactly like every general-purpose technology in history.

That doesn't mean the payoff is guaranteed. It means the timeline of your impatience is wrong.

### 4. You can't just plug a motor into an old factory

This is the deepest parallel, and the one that should change how you actually work.

Here's why the 30-year productivity void happened.

Pre-electric factories were built around **steam power**. A single giant steam engine in the basement drove overhead **line shafts** — long rotating shafts running the length of the ceiling. From these shafts, leather belts descended to individual machines. The entire factory was a fixed geometry of shafts, pulleys, and belts. If a machine wasn't near a shaft, it couldn't run. If a shaft broke, half the factory stopped.

When electricity arrived, the "obvious" thing to do — and what most factories did for the first 20 years — was to **replace the steam engine with one giant electric motor** driving the same line shafts.

You know what happened? Nothing. A little energy savings. Zero productivity revolution.

The revolution came only when factories were **rebuilt from scratch** around a new architecture called **unit drive**: one electric motor per machine. Each machine now had its own power source. Which meant — and this is where it gets interesting — **the factory floor was no longer constrained by shaft geometry**.

Machines could be arranged in the order of the *work*, not in the order of the *power supply*. Single-story linear factories became possible. Material could flow from raw input to finished product in a straight line. Overhead cranes could move heavy parts. And in 1913, Henry Ford put all of this together at Highland Park and created the moving assembly line.

**The productivity revolution wasn't the electric motor. It was the factory that was designed around the electric motor.**

Now translate this to AI. What are we mostly doing today? We are **bolting Copilot onto existing workflows**. We are adding AI to a Jira ticket flow that assumes a human writes each ticket. We are adding AI to a code review process that assumes a human wrote every line. We are adding AI to a support queue that assumes each ticket is a human's full attention.

**That is putting an electric motor on a steam-era line shaft.**

The productivity gains from AI will not come from AI copilots inside old workflows. They will come from workflows — from *companies* — that are architected from the ground up assuming AI is a factor of production. The org chart itself has to change. The definition of a "task" has to change. The unit of work that gets budgeted, measured, and shipped has to change.

Almost no one is doing this yet. That's why the ROI numbers look bad. That's why they'll keep looking bad — until the first companies that rebuild from scratch start eating the ones that didn't.

### 5. Ford, not GE, captured the value

Here is the twist that ties it all together.

The 1920s productivity boom that David describes — the one that came at the end of the 30-year lag — did **not** primarily enrich the power companies. GE and Westinghouse did fine. But the trillion-dollar winner of the electricity revolution wasn't the utilities.

It was **the industries that redesigned themselves around electricity**.

Ford. General Motors. Sears. The consumer packaged-goods empires that only became possible once factories could produce cheaply at scale and railroads could distribute cheaply nationally. Ford's assembly line arrived in **1913** — thirty-one years after Edison's Pearl Street Station opened in 1882. Ford didn't invent anything electrical. He redesigned the factory.

And *he* captured the value.

If the analogy holds, the AI winners of the 2030s are not going to be OpenAI or Anthropic — as impressive as those companies are. They're going to be companies you may not have heard of yet, whose entire operating model is native to AI as a factor of production. The way Ford was native to electricity in a way GE never could be.

This has an implication for individuals, too. **The most valuable career move of this decade is probably not "get better at AI tools."** It's "get good at redesigning workflows and organizations around AI." That's the Ford move. Everyone teaching prompt engineering right now is teaching people to be electricians. The Fords of the 2030s are learning something different.

## What to do with this on Monday

Three shifts, in order of importance.

**1. Stop measuring AI on quarterly timelines.**
You are running a 30-year process. If your CFO is asking "did the Claude license pay for itself last quarter," the answer isn't a spreadsheet — it's a history lesson. The right time horizon for judging AI ROI at the company level is *years*, not quarters. At the individual level: are you a materially different professional than you were 12 months ago? That's the only quarterly ROI question that matters.

**2. Ask yourself: am I bolting AI onto an old workflow, or redesigning around it?**
Take one process you own. Not "how do I add AI to it," but: *if I were starting this process from scratch today, assuming AI as a factor of production, would it look anything like what I have now?* If the answer is "yes, mostly the same," you are almost certainly leaving the real gains on the table. This is the single most useful question I've learned to ask this year.

**3. Position yourself as a Ford, not an Edison, not a Tesla.**
Edisons invented and got squeezed out. Teslas invented and gave the value away. Fords didn't invent anything — they *rearchitected* around what others invented, and they captured a generation of value. In your job, in your company, in whatever you're building: which of these three are you? If you're not sure, you're probably an Edison who thinks he's a Ford.

## The uncomfortable coda

There is a version of this essay that ends with "so relax, everything is fine, AI is just following the electricity playbook."

That's not quite the takeaway.

The electricity revolution *did* pay off. But the 30-year void wasn't painless. It contained a massive speculative bubble — Samuel Insull's utility empire ran up 85+ companies, saw its stock 5x in eight months of 1929, and collapsed into $800 million of receivership by April 1932. It contained the destruction of the mid-skill craftsman class — labor polarization that hollowed out artisanal work within a decade of electricity's arrival in energy-intensive industries. It contained bankruptcies, layoffs, communities that never recovered when their industry got reorganized around the new factor of production.

The technology paid off in aggregate. **Individuals and companies who didn't reposition in time did not.**

So the historical lesson is not "don't worry." It's:

> **In the middle of a general-purpose technology revolution, the winners are the ones who use the 30-year void to redesign, and the losers are the ones who use it to wait.**

You are, right now, in year three of the void. It is not going to feel productive for a while. That doesn't mean nothing is happening.

The Fords of 2050 are already at work. They are the ones who, when everyone else is asking "when will AI pay off," are quietly asking a different question:

*"What does my factory look like if I build it from scratch?"*

That's the question. That's the whole question.

Good luck.
